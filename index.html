What role does infrastructure play in crime?

Let's explore this question using data from the city of Chicago.

Why this idea?

We are interested in the role that infrastructure plays in inner-city crime. Our curiosity led us to develop this interactive visualization where users can explore crime in the city of Chicago and see when it occurs near broken street lights. This project is just one step towards understanding the effects infrastructure has on a community. By better understanding the impacts of infrastructure on urban crime we can build safer cities and neighborhoods.

The Data

Our data was sourced from the City of Chicago's open data portal. We combined two datasets, one containing crime data for the city and the other containing repair requests for broken street lights. In this stitching process we determined if a crime occurred near a broken street light or not. Here, we define "near" as within 100 feet. Because of the size of these data and the varying levels of completeness, we chose to focus our efforts on 2012, the most recent year with infrastructure data.

First Thing's First

Take a look below to familiarize yourself with some details about Chicago, the crime there, and the street lights

Crime Occurrence By Week

This graph shows crime occurrence in Chicago by time of year. In addition, you can filter it by crime and whether you only want to look at crimes that occur at a certain time of day. Use this graph to explore seasonality in Chicago crime occurrences.

Crime by Time of Day

This interactive map allows you to explore where in the city certain crimes occur. Additionally you can use the show history feature to keep a running log of where crimes occurred throughout the day. Protip: try combining Show History with the Day or Night checkboxes.

2012 Chicago Crime & Street Lights

This interactive chart allows you to navigate through time and explore various crime occurrences in the city of Chicago and whether they occurred near a burnt out streetlight or not. Additionally, you can toggle whether to display crimes that occur just at night, during the day, or both.
Try finding clusters of crime near burnt out lights. Was there crime in the area before the lights burnt out? After? You may be on track to finding evidence of the lights influencing crime!

Conclusion

Research reports correlations between infrastructure and crime. The results, however, are inconclusive in regards to the effect of street lights on crime. Our exploration above reveals several neighborhoods that show signs of an effect, but the overall results are largely inconclusive. This illustrates the complexity of the issue and that further research and better data collection is necessary to reveal the true answer to this question.
